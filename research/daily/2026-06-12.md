### 1. Factor Timing via Momentum-Based Regime Switching (Tai, Leung, Jimenez 2026)
- **Score**: 34 (Low Confidence) | latent 34 (capped to 34 — evidence auditability 2/10,
  SSRN working paper only, max drawdown NOT REPORTED, costs NOT MODELED, Dichtl 2019
  cautionary precedent)
- **Asset class**: US Equity Factor Indices (long-only multi-factor)
- **Timeframe**: Monthly (implied)
- **Core concept**: Tai, Leung, Jimenez (SSRN 6224058, Feb 2026) identify Bull/Bear regimes
  for each of 5 US equity factors (Value, Size, Momentum, Quality, Growth) using a z-score
  of each factor's trend signal with 2 hyperparameters. Bull-regime factors are overweighted;
  Bear-regime factors receive lower/equal weight. Claims Sharpe 0.66 vs. 0.59 EW benchmark
  and CAGR 13.0% vs. 11.3% over 1998–2025 (27 years), with 78.6% calendar year wins.
- **Evidence status**: CLAIMED, UNVERIFIED — SSRN working paper; primary paper HTTP 403;
  all metrics from secondary search summaries; no peer review; no open code
- **Source links**:
  - [SSRN 6224058 — Tai/Leung/Jimenez (2026)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6224058) — HTTP 403
- **Verification level**: None — working paper; primary paper inaccessible; no open code
- **Analyst note**: Factor timing has a notoriously poor out-of-sample track record.
  Dichtl et al. (2019, FAJ) specifically found that momentum-based factor timing approaches
  fail after transaction costs. The Sharpe improvement is modest (0.59→0.66) with marginal
  statistical significance (t=1.98, p=0.047). Maximum drawdown is NOT REPORTED, making
  the risk-adjusted comparison incomplete. Closest related entry in our database:
  `dynamic-factor-allocation-regime-shu-mulvey` (score 61) — superior evidence (peer-reviewed
  JPM 2025) and superior mechanism (SJM + Black-Litterman vs. z-score).

### 2. Fractional Momentum — Smoothing Out Momentum and Reversal
- **Score**: 52 (Experimental) | latent 52 (capped to 52 — evidence auditability 3/10,
  SFI working papers not peer-reviewed after 3+ years on SSRN; absolute Sharpe NOT REPORTED;
  optimal d NOT REPORTED; constraint details inaccessible)
- **Asset class**: US Equities (cross-sectional long-short); international markets claimed
- **Timeframe**: Daily rebalancing (effective monthly-equivalent turnover via path constraints)
- **Core concept**: Two SFI working papers by Chitsiripanich, Paolella, Polak, Walker
  (UZH/Stony Brook). Paper 1 (SSRN 4280465, 2022): fractional differencing parameter d ∈
  [0,1] blends classical momentum (d=1) and reversal (d=0), using exponentially decaying
  weights across the entire lookback period to extract long-memory signals. Paper 2 (SSRN
  4955388, 2024): adds path-dependent constraints in sequential portfolio optimization, reducing
  daily-rebalanced turnover by 95–99%. Claims: max drawdown reduced from 76–99% to 22–49%;
  risk-adjusted net returns improved 38–149%; costs explicitly modeled.
- **Evidence status**: CLAIMED, UNVERIFIED — SFI working papers; neither paper peer-reviewed
  as of 2026-06-12 (3.5 years on SSRN); all metrics from secondary search summaries;
  absolute Sharpe ratio NOT REPORTED in any accessible source
- **Source links**:
  - [SSRN 4280465 — Momentum Without Crashes (2022)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4280465) — HTTP 403
  - [SSRN 4955388 — Smoothing Out Momentum and Reversal (2024)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4955388) — HTTP 403
- **Verification level**: None — SFI working papers; primary papers blocked; no open code
- **Analyst note**: The most important fact about this pair of papers is that they have
  been on SSRN for 3+ years (2022 paper) without journal publication. The core improvement
  may be trivially explained by comparing to daily-rebalanced momentum (which no practitioner
  implements) rather than the fair baseline of monthly-rebalanced momentum. Transaction costs
  ARE modeled — this is rare and commendable. But the absolute Sharpe ratio is absent from
  all accessible sources, which is a significant red flag. Score 52 reflects the
  positive features (costs modeled, DD explicitly stated, multi-market claimed) tempered by
  the evidence quality ceiling (no peer review, no code, no absolute performance numbers).

## Emerging Patterns

- **Factor timing proliferation, quality varies**: We now have three entries on US equity
  factor timing/rotation: `dynamic-factor-allocation-regime-shu-mulvey` (61, Worth Researching,
  peer-reviewed), `systematic-reversal-industry-momentum` (36, Low Confidence), and now
  `factor-timing-momentum-regime-tai` (34, Low Confidence). The pattern is clear: simple
  momentum-following regime detection on US factors produces statistically marginal improvements
  that don't survive scrutiny once transaction costs and max drawdown are examined.
- **SSRN access remains blocked**: All SSRN direct fetch attempts returned HTTP 403 this run.
  This continues to be the binding constraint on research quality. Secondary search engine
  summaries are the only source for quantitative claims.
- **Factor timing literature consensus**: The Dichtl et al. (2019, FAJ) paper is the
  clearest precedent: factor timing with momentum signals is a "cautionary tale" after
  transaction costs. New papers continue to propose similar approaches with marginal
  improvements, but none from accessible sources demonstrate robust OOS evidence.
- **SFI working paper longevity as a quality signal**: The fractional momentum papers (2022)
  have been on SSRN for 3.5 years without peer review acceptance. This run reinforces the
  observation that SSRN residence time is a useful (inverse) proxy for evidence quality.
  Papers that represent genuine advances typically clear peer review within 1-2 years. Papers
  that persist longer often have methodological issues that reviewers have identified. This
  pattern should be applied consistently in future screening decisions.
- **Absolute Sharpe ratio as a disclosure standard**: Two consecutive strategies this run
  had no accessible absolute Sharpe ratio — only relative improvement claims. This is a
  methodological red flag. Papers that show only "X% better than baseline" without disclosing
  the actual Sharpe allow cherry-picking of bad baselines. Future screening should downgrade
  papers that omit absolute risk-adjusted return figures.
