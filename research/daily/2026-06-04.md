## Strategies This Pass (ranked by confidence)

### 1. Short-Term Basis Reversal in Commodity Futures — 54 (Experimental)

- **Asset class:** Commodity Futures (Agriculture, Energy, Metals — 39 contracts, Norgate Data)
- **Timeframe:** Weekly (1-week holding period)
- **Core concept:** The spread return between the front-month (M1) and second-month (M2) commodity futures
  contract exhibits systematic negative weekly autocorrelation. Front-month contracts overreact to news
  relative to second-month contracts; the spread then reverts. Cross-sectional strategy: rank 39 commodities
  by prior-week M1−M2 spread return; go long 4 spreads with the most negative prior return (expecting
  rebound) and short 4 spreads with the most positive prior return (expecting reversal). Weekly rebalancing.
- **Evidence status:** `CLAIMED, UNVERIFIED` — SSRN working paper (Georgetown); not peer-reviewed; no
  open code confirmed
- **Source links:**
  - https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5250499 (Rossi/Zhang/Zhu, May 2025)
  - https://quantreturns.com/strategy-review/short-term-basis-reversal/ (independent impl. review)
  - https://substack.com/@quantitativo/note/c-117932838 (Quantitativo commentary)
- **Verification level:** Low–moderate. SSRN working paper from credible institution (Georgetown
  McDonough). Independent QuantReturns replication on 2007–2025 confirms directional finding (Sharpe 0.92
  vs. 0.62 benchmark). Paper not peer-reviewed; no open code; cost treatment unconfirmed.
- **Analyst note:** The strategy is attractively simple (one signal, one parameter), market-neutral, and
  diversified across 39 commodities. The economic rationale (differential information-speed across the
  curve) is falsifiable and cross-asset (bonds and equity index futures show the same pattern). The main
  concern is the unexplained gap between paper performance (17.6%/1.42) and independent implementation
  (9.68%/0.92). This is consistent with either a pre-cost vs. post-cost distinction, regime decay, or a
  more favorable historical period included in the paper's "several decades." The strategy would rank
  higher if the paper were peer-reviewed and transaction costs explicitly stated.

### 2. Dynamic Factor Allocation Leveraging Regime-Switching Signals — 61 (Worth Researching)

- **Asset class:** US Equities — 6 style factor indices (value, size, momentum, quality, low volatility, growth)
- **Timeframe:** Monthly rebalancing (inferred)
- **Core concept:** The Sparse Jump Model (SJM) identifies factor-specific bull/bear regimes for each of the
  6 US style factors. These regime signals are fed into the Black-Litterman framework as views to overweight
  factors in bull regimes and underweight factors in bear regimes, constructing a fully-invested, long-only
  multi-factor portfolio. Information ratio improved from 0.05 (equal-weight benchmark) to ~0.4.
- **Evidence status:** `CLAIMED, UNVERIFIED` for full metrics (paywalled); but peer-reviewed in JPM Vol.
  51 Issue 3, 2025 — methodology validated by peer review. The `jumpmodels` Python package is openly
  available on PyPI/GitHub for the SJM component.
- **Source links:**
  - https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.14841 (Shu/Mulvey, arXiv 2410.14841)
  - https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4960484 (SSRN 4960484)
  - https://github.com/Yizhan-Oliver-Shu/jump-models (jumpmodels GitHub)
  - https://pypi.org/project/jumpmodels/ (PyPI)
- **Verification level:** Moderate-high methodology quality (JPM peer review). But absolute performance
  metrics (Sharpe, CAGR, drawdown) are paywalled. Only IR improvement (0.05→0.4) accessible from search
  result summaries.
- **Analyst note:** This is the second paper from the Princeton Shu/Mulvey group applying the Statistical
  Jump Model to portfolio management (we already have `regime-switching-jump-model-equity` in our database).
  The key novelty: instead of market timing (in/out of equity), this paper applies SJM to *factor* timing
  (which style factor to overweight). The IR improvement from 0.05 to 0.4 is meaningful for active equity
  management. The strategy ranks as Worth Researching primarily due to JPM peer review and the open
  `jumpmodels` codebase. Major caveat: absolute performance is paywalled and the comparison is against a
  weak EW benchmark rather than factor-momentum strategies.

## Emerging Patterns

- **M1−M2 spread dynamics as a signal source:** This is the first strategy in our database that specifically
  exploits the *difference* between front-month and second-month futures pricing as a directional signal
  (beyond carry, which captures the *level* of the term structure). The mechanism (differential news
  incorporation speed across the curve) is novel relative to existing strategies.
- **Princeton SJM group expansion:** The Shu/Mulvey/Kolm research program now has three strategies in our
  database (regime-switching-jump-model-equity, and now dynamic-factor-allocation-regime-shu-mulvey). The
  related dynamic-asset-allocation paper (arXiv 2406.09578) on multi-asset TAA is a candidate for future
  research. This group is building a coherent, peer-reviewed framework for regime-based portfolio
  management with open-source tooling.
- **Performance gap between SSRN claims and independent replications:** Now seen repeatedly in this
  research database. The systematic downward revision from paper to independent implementation is
  significant enough to be a standing workflow step: always check for independent implementations when
  evaluating an SSRN working paper.

## Red Flags Detected

- Paper claims 17.6–18% annual returns and Sharpe 1.42; independent implementation for 2007–2025 shows
  9.68%/0.92 — a ~40% reduction in both metrics. This gap is inadequately explained by available sources.
- "Pre-cost Sharpe ratios above 1" language may mean the headline 1.42 is pre-cost; net performance unknown.
- "Several decades" is vague — the paper may rely on a historical period with unusually high commodity curve
  volatility (e.g. 2000s supercycle) that inflated the headline metrics.
- Not peer-reviewed; no open code.
