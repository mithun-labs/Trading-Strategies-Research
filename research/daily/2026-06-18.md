## Strategies This Pass (ranked by confidence)

### 1. Maxing Out Short-Term Reversals in Weekly Stock Returns — Chen, Cohen, Liang, Sun (JEF Vol. 82, 2025)

- **Score:** 44 (Experimental) | latent 44 (evidence auditability 6/10: peer-reviewed JEF,
  methodology documented but full paper paywalled; no open code; cap at 74 not binding since
  latent 44 < 74)
- **Asset class:** US Equities (NYSE + AMEX + NASDAQ, CRSP; July 1963–Dec 2022)
- **Timeframe:** Weekly rebalancing (5-day hold period)
- **Core concept:** Each week, sort stocks by MAX (maximum daily return from the week before
  last, week t−2) into quintiles; within each MAX quintile, implement a standard short-term
  reversal (long past-week losers, short past-week winners). The high-MAX (lottery stock)
  reversal generates 1.66%/week gross vs. 0.65%/week for low-MAX stocks. Mechanism: retail
  lottery-seeking investors overreact to news → larger dislocations → stronger reversals.
  Effect only significant when retail order imbalance is in highest quintile.
- **Evidence status:** CLAIMED, UNVERIFIED — despite JEF peer review, performance numbers
  are gross returns from a backtest on CRSP data; full paper paywalled; no net-of-cost figures
  from accessible sources
- **Source links:**
  - SSRN 4622831: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4622831
  - ScienceDirect: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927539825000301
  - IDEAS/RePEC: https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/empfin/v82y2025ics0927539825000301.html
- **Verification level:** Peer-reviewed (JEF, Elsevier; B+/A− tier empirical finance); Old
  Dominion University authors; no independent replication found
- **Analyst note:** The behavioral mechanism (lottery demand → overreaction → reversal) is
  coherent and falsifiable. However, the score is suppressed primarily by the missing cost
  analysis. High-MAX stocks are lottery/small-cap names with bid-ask spreads of 200–500bps
  round-trip per week — against a gross edge of 1.66%/week, this is likely fatal. The Hou/
  Xue/Zhang (2020) precedent showing the baseline VW reversal ≈ zero provides further adverse
  context. The MAX filter rescues the equal-weighted version but concentrates the portfolio in
  the precise illiquid segment where costs are highest. This is not an implementation candidate
  (latent 44 < 60 threshold). Accessing the full paper to find net-of-cost analysis is the
  single most important next step.

## Emerging Patterns

- **Illiquidity premium vs. tradable alpha (recurring theme):** Three of the last four
  strategies researched involve mechanisms that are fundamentally illiquidity or risk premia,
  not behavioral alpha net of frictions:
  1. `crypto-drl-pairs-slepaczuk` — crypto pairs stats arb; funding rates not modeled
  2. `intramonth-momentum-pretom` — liquidity provision around month-end obligations
  3. `maxing-out-reversal-max-stocks` — short-term reversal in illiquid lottery stocks
  A recurring lesson: gross returns in academic studies of microstructure-driven anomalies
  are almost always pre-cost; net returns after realistic market impact and bid-ask frequently
  invert sign.

- **Lottery-stock literature maturing:** The MAX effect (Bali/Cakici/Whitelaw 2011) has
  spawned a generation of papers applying MAX as a screening variable. Most find gross
  improvements but few survive rigorous cost analysis. The Chen et al. paper is a well-
  executed example of this tradition but does not break the cost-analysis barrier.

- **JEF peer-review quality is solid but not sufficient for cost claims:** Journal of
  Empirical Finance applies good methodology standards, but peer reviewers do not require
  net-of-cost analysis for cross-sectional stock studies. Publication ≠ cost viability.

- **HTTP 403 barrier persisting (9th consecutive run):** All primary SSRN PDFs and most
  ScienceDirect articles continue to return 403. Research quality is systematically limited
