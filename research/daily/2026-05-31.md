### 1. FX Carry + Value + Momentum — 200+ Year History
- **Score:** 51 — EXPERIMENTAL
- **Asset class:** G10 FX / Developed market currencies
- **Timeframe:** Monthly rebalancing
- **Core concept:** Tests three canonical FX factors over 200+ years of data (from 1788): carry (long high-yield currencies, short low-yield), momentum (long past winners, short losers), and value/reversal. Key findings: carry is robust across centuries (Sharpe 0.391 for short-rate carry; 0.361 for long-bond carry), momentum only works in the latter half of the 200-year sample, and value/reversal is confined to the post-Bretton Woods period only. Cross-currency yield-curve flattening identified as the most historically robust strategy.
- **Evidence status:** CLAIMED, UNVERIFIED — SSRN working paper (Joseph Chen, revised 2024); not peer-reviewed; Quantpedia blog summary; all direct sources HTTP 403
- **Source links:** [Quantpedia blog](https://quantpedia.com/fx-carry-value-momentum-strategies-over-their-200-year-history/) · [Quantpedia Medium](https://quantpedia.medium.com/fx-carry-value-momentum-strategies-over-their-200-year-history-a2c13cb5af1e)
- **Verification level:** SSRN working paper only; no peer review; no open code; no independent replication
- **Analyst note:** The Sharpe ratios for carry (0.39/0.36) are modest and consistent with crash-risk compensation rather than unexploited alpha. More importantly, the paper explicitly shows that momentum and value don't survive the full 200-year test — this is the most valuable finding. The Rohrbach/Suremann (2017) research corroborates this: G10 momentum stopped working after the 2008 GFC. The 200-year framework is methodologically sound but the carry edge is well-known and the new deployable signal (yield-curve flattening) is described but not fully specified. Score 51: strong historical perspective, honest negative results, but SSRN-only and no cost modeling.

### 2. Currency Momentum Factor — Cross-Sectional FX Momentum
- **Score:** 59 — EXPERIMENTAL (highest latent in Experimental band; no cap applies)
- **Asset class:** ~48 currencies (G10 + emerging markets, USD-based)
- **Timeframe:** Monthly (1-month formation, 1-month holding — MOM(1,1))
- **Core concept:** Cross-sectional momentum on 48 currencies. Sort by f-month past return; long top quintile, short bottom quintile; equal-weight; monthly rebalancing. Five formation periods (1, 3, 6, 9, 12 months) all produce positive in-sample excess returns. MOM(1,1) is best in-sample: Sharpe 0.95, up to 10% p.a. spread between winners and losers, 1976–2010. Critical: costs significantly erode profitability; momentum portfolios are skewed toward illiquid currencies.
- **Evidence status:** AUDITABLE (in-sample) + AUDITABLE OOS FAILURE — JFE Vol 106 (2012) for in-sample; Hutchinson et al. IRFA 2022 (peer-reviewed) for OOS decay: Sharpe dropped from +0.39 to −0.32 post-publication; G10 momentum stopped post-GFC (Rohrbach/Suremann 2017)
- **Source links:** [SSRN 1988679](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1988679) · [BIS WP 366](https://www.bis.org/publ/work366.htm) · [Hutchinson et al. SSRN 4024296](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4024296)
- **Verification level:** Peer-reviewed JFE (top journal); peer-reviewed OOS decay (IRFA 2022); independently corroborated by multiple studies
- **Analyst note:** The JFE 2012 paper is textbook academic finance — peer-reviewed, 34 years, 48 currencies, multiple formation periods. The in-sample Sharpe of 0.95 was real. But the post-publication Sharpe of −0.32 is equally real. This is the cleanest publication-effect story in the database: the strategy worked until it was published, then arbitrageurs removed the mispricing. The emerging-market and crypto versions may still hold some edge (higher friction = less arbitrage), but G10 FX momentum appears to be exhausted. Score 59 reflects the JFE evidence quality while the robustness dimension (3/10) captures the OOS failure.

## Emerging Patterns

1. **Carry as the only robust long-run FX factor:** This run confirms convergent evidence from two independent papers. Chen (2024) shows carry works over 200 years; Menkhoff et al. (2012) shows momentum's in-sample Sharpe (0.95) is higher but doesn't survive OOS. The surviving FX risk premium is carry, not momentum.

2. **Post-publication decay is a documented and peer-reviewed phenomenon in FX:** The Hutchinson et al. 2022 (IRFA) paper provides the most direct evidence in the database that a published strategy was arbitraged away. OOS Sharpe went from +0.39 to −0.32. This pattern — robust in-sample, weak or negative OOS after publication — is what we should expect from any academically-publicized FX anomaly.

3. **Honest answer to "high win rate + good risk-to-reward" in forex:** The research this pass reinforces the prior analysis. Carry (~60% monthly direction accuracy) is the closest to "high win rate" in academic FX evidence, but comes with documented negative skewness (crash risk). Momentum had the higher single-Sharpe but has decayed to negative. **There is no documented, currently active FX strategy in the academic literature that satisfies both "high win rate" AND "good risk-to-reward" simultaneously without either (a) being period-specific, or (b) being net-negative after costs in OOS testing.** The user's request points at a genuinely hard problem.

## Research Context: User Request

The user specifically requested a YouTube forex strategy with **high win rate and good
risk-to-reward ratio**. The ICT Silver Bullet is the most prominent such strategy in the
community. Key finding: **the high win rate claim (70–80%) is structurally suspect** for
the following reasons:

1. Win rates this high on a discretionary 1-min scalping strategy, if genuine, would be
   visible in audited prop-firm payouts and Robbins Cup entries. Neither exists for this strategy.
2. The only audited performance data for the strategy's creator runs in the opposite direction.
3. "Selectively profitable" and "pre-2023 varies widely" are community admissions of
   inconsistency that are inconsistent with a genuine 70–80% edge.

**What the research DID find worth noting:**
- The killzone timing (session windows) has genuine microstructure support
- FVGs as a price pattern show partial statistical backing (66–76% fill rates in limited studies)
- The 1:2 minimum R:R structure is well-designed
- For a sufficiently mechanical version (automate FVG + killzone filter; remove MSS discretion),
  there may be a testable hypothesis worth pursuing — but it would need a proper mechanical
  backtest on historical 1-min data

## Emerging Patterns

1. **YouTube strategy creators' live track records are disqualifying evidence.** Michael
   Huddleston is not the first case: the pattern of high win-rate claims on YouTube with
   no auditable live results is structural. Going forward, any YouTube-sourced strategy
   should immediately trigger a search for the creator's live trading record before
   spending additional research time on the setup logic.

2. **The session-timing effect is a legitimate separable hypothesis.** Multiple independent
   lines of evidence support that certain intraday windows (London Open, NY Open) produce
   higher-quality directional moves. This effect is separable from the ICT conceptual
   framework. A future research pass could evaluate the killzone timing hypothesis alone
   (does restricting entry to killzone hours improve an otherwise random entry on EURUSD?).

3. **FVG as a mean-reversion signal has partial independent support.** The 66–76% fill
   rates from limited studies suggest price imbalances (3-candle gaps) do tend to be filled.
   This is consistent with market microstructure research on price efficiency. However,
   fill rate ≠ strategy profitability — a 1-pip fill at the 50% level of a 10-pip FVG is
   not the same as a profitable trade.
