## Strategies This Pass (ranked by confidence)

### 1. Multi-Asset Pullback Strategy (200-MA Uptrend + Consecutive Down-Day Trigger)

- **Score:** 42 (Experimental) | latent 42 (cap 59 not binding; evidence auditability 2/10 — blog post with 600-test AI-assisted data mining; no peer review; no independent replication)
- **Asset class:** Multi-asset ETFs (SPY, EEM, IEF, FXE, GLD, DBC — equities, fixed income, FX, gold, commodities)
- **Timeframe:** Daily bars; 1-day holding period
- **Core concept:** Buy any ETF trading above its 200-day SMA after N consecutive days of negative returns; close the position at the next day's close. Position sizing is dynamically equal-weighted across simultaneous signals; a vol-targeted variant scales position as 10%/realized_vol. The strategy operates on the hypothesis that multi-day declines in uptrending assets are disproportionately caused by transient liquidity shocks (systematic de-risking, margin calls, ETF flow-driven selling) that revert within one trading day. The 200-day MA filter prevents trading against bear-market trends where declines are more likely fundamental.
- **Evidence status:** CLAIMED, UNVERIFIED — hypothetical backtest by Quantpedia junior analyst; 600-test AI-assisted grid search; no peer review; no independent replication; EODHD data
- **Source links:**
  - Quantpedia blog: https://quantpedia.com/testing-an-ai-assisted-research-workflow-for-multi-asset-pullback-strategy-discovery/
- **Verification level:** Internal Quantpedia analysis; not peer-reviewed; no external academic reference; data-mined from 600-test grid
- **Analyst note:** This strategy is mechanically equivalent to Larry Connors' RSI(2) framework — consecutive-down-days is a discrete analog of extreme RSI(2) oversold readings applied to a multi-asset ETF universe. Connors documented the same mechanism in 2008 ("Short Term Trading Strategies That Work") and it subsequently decayed in equity markets as the strategy became widely known. The 600-test grid search without multiple-testing correction is the dominant methodological flaw. ANALYSIS: transaction costs on these liquid ETFs are relatively low (~3bps round-trip average; estimated net CAGR reduction ~0.6% per year for the 3-day/1-day variant), so the strategy likely survives costs — but this remains unconfirmed. The sub-period analysis (all 4 five-year windows positive) and cross-asset diversification (6 ETF classes) are genuine positives, but they don't fully overcome the data-mining concern. Score: 42 (Experimental). NOT an implementation candidate (latent 42 < 60).

## Emerging Patterns

- **AI-assisted discovery as a genre:** The Quantpedia "Testing an AI-Assisted Research Workflow" article represents an emerging pattern in quant research — use of LLMs (Claude, ChatGPT) to assist in parameter grid searches and analysis, then publish the discovery as a blog post. This is transparent (the article explicitly states Claude 3 Opus and ChatGPT 5.5 were used) but raises systematic data-mining concerns across this category of research. The 600-test grid without multiple-testing correction is structurally no different from running 600 regressions and reporting only the significant one.

- **Connors RSI(2) in multi-asset form:** The strategy researched today is the third time in this database we encounter the "mean reversion within uptrend" mechanism: `intraday-momentum-spy` (score 50, different TF), `fast-alphas-intraday-overlay` (score 55, streak-based reversal on SPY intraday), and now `multi-asset-pullback-200ma` (score 42, consecutive-down-days on 6 ETFs). The pattern is well-established but consistently struggles with transaction costs, publication effects, and the difficulty of distinguishing genuine reversion from noise.

- **HTTP 403 barrier persisting:** For the 10th consecutive run, all primary SSRN PDFs and most journal articles return HTTP 403. Research quality continues to be bounded by this infrastructure constraint. Today's research was conducted entirely from accessible blog posts and arXiv abstracts.
